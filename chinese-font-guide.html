Universal CJK Rules
通用中文排版规则

Four rules that apply to every Chinese rendering, regardless of which font system you choose. These are the difference between Chinese type that looks designed and Chinese type that looks like it was rendered in a hurry.

RULE 01

Looser line-height · 加大行高

Chinese glyphs are square and visually full — they crowd vertically more than Latin. Body text needs line-height ≈ 1.7–1.85 (vs. 1.5–1.6 for Latin). Display text needs ≈ 1.15–1.25 (vs. 0.9–1.0 for Latin).

❌ Latin spec (1.2)
不要让汉字像英文一样紧凑排版，否则上下笔画很容易撞在一起，整体显得拥挤局促。
✅ CJK spec (1.7)
不要让汉字像英文一样紧凑排版，否则上下笔画很容易撞在一起，整体显得拥挤局促。
RULE 02

One font per sentence · 一句之内不混字体

Pick a CJK font that has decent Latin coverage, or pair it with a Latin face whose proportions match. Avoid having the browser pick a random fallback for Latin characters inside a Chinese sentence — that mid-sentence switch always looks broken.

❌ Mixed (browser font-switches inside the sentence)

✅ Single family with Latin coverage

RULE 03

No tracking · 不要字距

Letter-spacing that looks tight and editorial on Latin glyphs looks broken on square CJK characters. Tracking creates uneven gaps between strokes that the eye reads as random whitespace, not rhythm.

❌ With letter-spacing
中 文 字 体 不 应 该 使 用 字 距
✅ No letter-spacing
中文字体不应该使用字距
RULE 04

Full-width punctuation · 全角标点

Chinese uses full-width punctuation: ，。：；！？「」（）— never force half-width punctuation in Chinese content. Full-width marks carry their own spacing; mixing in half-width creates uneven gaps.

❌ Half-width punctuation
我们的产品,主要面向亚太市场:中国,日本,韩国.
✅ Full-width punctuation
我们的产品，主要面向亚太市场：中国、日本、韩国。
RULE 05

Space between CJK and Latin · 中英之间加空格

Insert a regular ASCII space between every Chinese character and adjacent Latin character or digit (sometimes called "盘古之白" / Pangu spacing). Without it, the eye can't separate words from glyphs and the line reads as crowded. The space also lets the browser pick better line-break points.

Mixed CJK + Latin Handling
中英混排处理

Real Chinese decks rarely contain pure Chinese — brand names, model names, technical terms, numbers, and units constantly mix in. How those mixed sentences render depends entirely on the font-family chain you set. There are three viable strategies and several traps. The demos below use realistic content from the kinds of decks this library produces (AI, product, financial, technical).

Recommendation · 推荐

Default (90% of templates)

Strategy A — Single CJK family

Use 'Noto Sans SC' or 'Noto Serif SC' as the sole family. Both have full Latin coverage. Consistent across platforms, no baseline issues, no font-switching.

For literary / editorial templates

Strategy C — Latin first, CJK fallback

'Latin Font', 'Noto Sans/Serif SC', fallback. Use only when the template's Latin face has a strong identity that you want to preserve for English words. Inspect display headlines carefully for baseline mismatch.

Hard rule: Never use a CJK font that lacks Latin coverage (like 站酷快乐体, 马善政毛笔楷书) for any text that contains Latin characters. Reserve those decorative faces for pure-Chinese headlines.

Licensing summary

OFL-1.1 (SIL Open Font License): free use, modification, redistribution — most fonts here.

免费商用 (Free for commercial use): vendor-specific permission, usually no modification of font itself.

IPA-1.0: Japanese IPA license, free use and distribution.

Pairing principles (from the guide)

Contrast for hierarchy: title and body from different style categories (script + sans, art + serif).

Restraint for harmony: max 2-3 Chinese fonts per slide.

Clear role: title creates memory/emotion; body ensures readability. Choose by role first.

Install-only fonts — when to use

Some popular Chinese display faces (优设标题黑, 鸿蒙字体, MiSans, 演示魁本楷, etc.) have no public CDN. They only work if:

① You self-host: download the font file (.ttf/.woff2) and serve it from your own server via @font-face. Check the font's license first — most "免费商用" licenses permit self-hosting.

② You control the viewing environment: only viable for decks shown on your own machine where you've installed the font.

For shareable HTML decks (sent to others, embedded in pages, hosted online), stick to the CDN-loadable fonts in the table above. They'll render consistently for everyone.
